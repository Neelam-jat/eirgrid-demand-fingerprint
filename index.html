EirGrid demand · 2014–2023 · 355,000 readings

Ireland's nights are disappearing.

Not the dark. The quiet. I pulled eleven years of the country's electricity demand, every fifteen minutes, and went looking for who is awake at four in the morning.

scroll↓
The data

One number, 355,000 times.

Every fifteen minutes, EirGrid writes down how much electricity the whole island is pulling, in megawatts. That is all it is. No labels, no names, no list of who is using what. Just the heartbeat of a country, going back to 2014.

So the question gets simple and a bit annoying. If I cant see who is using the power, can I still work out what changed? You can. You just have to stop asking who, and start asking when.

The trend

First, the boring truth. Demand went up.

Average demand climbed about a quarter in nine years. Fine. Everyone knew that. But an average is a liar by omission, it never tells you when in the day the new hunger showed up. And the when is the entire story.

The cut

So I split the day in two.

Take the sleepy hours, three to five in the morning, when the country is unconscious. Call that the floor. Take the loud evening, five to seven, everyone home, kettles on, telly on. Call that the peak.

Humans drive the peak. Humans sleep straight through the floor. So if the floor is climbing fast, something that doesnt sleep is doing the climbing.

The floor grew almost twice as fast as the peak. Whatever is behind this does not go to bed.

The fingerprint

Here is the whole thing in one picture.

Every row is an hour of the day, midnight at the top. Every column is a year, 2014 on the left. The brighter a cell, the closer that hour sits to the day's own peak. Let your eye run along the dead of night, the three-to-five band, from left to right.

The dark is filling in. The nights are heating up.

The consequence

Now the part that should worry a grid operator.

I taught a standard forecasting model the old Ireland, only the years 2015 through 2019. Then I asked it to predict years it had never seen, and measured how far off it landed.

In 2019 it is nearly perfect. By 2023 it is wrong by seventy five megawatts, every hour, always too low, never too high. A miss that only ever leans one direction is not bad luck. It is a model faithfully predicting a country that no longer exists.

Pressure test

Three ways I tried to kill this finding.

"Offices are polluting your night floor." Redid the whole thing using weekends only, when the offices are shut. Same thirty nine percent.

"It is a fluke." The trend has about a one in forty thousand chance of being random noise. It is not noise.

"You are reading data centres into it." The official statisticians, counting real meters, say data centres caused 88 percent of Ireland's demand growth since 2015. I landed on the same answer from the shape of the curve alone, without ever seeing a single meter. It held.

So what

What this actually means.

01

Ireland is the extreme case.

Data centres here already eat roughly a fifth of the country's electricity, the highest share anywhere. New ones around Dublin are paused until 2028 because the grid cant take them.

02

The grid was built for a country that slept.

It doesnt anymore. The quiet hours that every forecast and every maintenance window quietly depended on are being eaten by load that runs flat out at four in the morning.

03

Every model trained on the old pattern is now wrong.

Consistently, in one direction. The fix is not better hardware. It is admitting the past stopped predicting the future, and rebuilding around demand that never switches off. That is the open problem. That is the interesting bit.
